Relativistic Momentum & Energy

By the end of this lesson you will be able to: calculate relativistic momentum, kinetic energy, total energy, and rest energy; apply $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$; and explain the significance of $E = mc^2$.

1. Why Classical Momentum Fails

Classical momentum $p = mu$ (mass × velocity) is not conserved in all inertial frames under the Lorentz transformation. We need a new definition that is.

2. Relativistic Momentum

Relativistic Momentum p = γmu

p = mu / √(1 − u²/c²)

u = velocity of the particle (not the frame speed v)
Note: As $u \to c$, $\gamma \to \infty$, so $p \to \infty$. This is why no massive object can reach the speed of light — it would require infinite momentum (and energy).

At low speeds ($u \ll c$): $\gamma \approx 1$, so $p \approx mu$ — classical result recovered.

3. Relativistic Kinetic Energy

Using the work-energy theorem with relativistic momentum (doing the calculus from Lec04):

Relativistic Kinetic Energy K = (γ − 1)mc²

K = mc²/√(1−u²/c²) − mc²
Classical limit check: At low speed, $\gamma \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{u^2}{c^2}$, so:
$K = (\gamma - 1)mc^2 \approx \frac{1}{2}mu^2$ ✓ — matches the classical formula.

4. Total Energy and Rest Energy

Total Relativistic Energy E = γmc² = K + mc²
Rest Energy (v = 0, γ = 1) E₀ = mc²

The total energy has two parts:

  • Rest energy $E_0 = mc^2$ — energy locked in the mass itself, even when stationary.
  • Kinetic energy $K = (\gamma-1)mc^2$ — energy due to motion.
$E = mc^2$ in context: This famous equation only applies to an object at rest ($v = 0$). The full equation for a moving object is $E = \gamma mc^2$.

5. Energy–Momentum Relation

By combining $E = \gamma mc^2$ and $p = \gamma mu$, we can eliminate $\gamma$ to get a powerful relation that works for all particles, including photons:

Energy–Momentum Invariant E² = (pc)² + (mc²)²

Special cases:

  • Massive particle at rest ($p = 0$): $E^2 = (mc^2)^2$, so $E = mc^2$ ✓
  • Photon ($m = 0$): $E^2 = (pc)^2$, so $E = pc$ ✓
Photons carry momentum: From $E = pc$, a photon with energy $E$ has momentum $p = E/c$. This means light can exert a force — it is the basis of radiation pressure, solar sails, and (in the next lesson) the photoelectric effect. This is the bridge from special relativity into quantum mechanics.

6. Real-World Application — GPS and Relativity

GPS satellites orbit at ~20,200 km altitude, moving at ~3.87 km/s. Two relativistic effects shift their clocks every day:

GPS Clock Corrections (per day) Special Relativity (time dilation — satellite moves fast):
   Satellite clock runs slow by ~7 μs/day

General Relativity (gravitational time dilation — weaker gravity at altitude):
   Satellite clock runs fast by ~45 μs/day

Net effect: satellite clock runs fast by ~38 μs/day

Position error if uncorrected: 38 μs × 3×10⁸ m/s ≈ 11.4 km/day
GPS proves relativity is real engineering, not just theory. Without the SR and GR corrections applied to every satellite, your phone's GPS would drift by over 11 km per day.

7. A Glimpse at General Relativity

Special Relativity covers inertial frames (constant velocity). General Relativity (Einstein, 1915) extends this to accelerating frames and gravity.

Key Ideas of General Relativity Equivalence Principle: gravity and acceleration are locally indistinguishable.
(A person in a closed box cannot tell if they are on Earth or in a rocket accelerating at g.)

Consequence: gravity bends light. The Sun deflects starlight by 1.75 arcseconds — confirmed by Eddington's 1919 solar eclipse expedition.

Mass curves spacetime. More mass = greater curvature = stronger "gravity well."
Black holes: curvature so extreme that escape speed > c → nothing escapes.

8. All Formulas at a Glance

Quantity Formula When classical = this
Momentum $p$ $p = \gamma mu$ $\gamma \to 1 \Rightarrow p = mu$
Kinetic energy $K$ $K = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$ $\approx \frac{1}{2}mu^2$ at low $u$
Rest energy $E_0$ $E_0 = mc^2$ No classical equivalent
Total energy $E$ $E = \gamma mc^2 = K + mc^2$ $\approx mc^2 + \frac{1}{2}mu^2$ at low $u$
Energy–momentum $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ Frame-independent invariant

9. Worked Example

An electron (mass $m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg) moves at $u = 0.8c$. Find its momentum, kinetic energy, and total energy.

γ = 1/√(1 − 0.64) = 1/0.6 = 5/3 ≈ 1.667

p = γm_e·u = (5/3)(9.11×10⁻³¹)(0.8 × 3×10⁸)
= (5/3)(9.11×10⁻³¹)(2.4×10⁸) ≈ 3.64×10⁻²² kg·m/s

E₀ = m_e c² = (9.11×10⁻³¹)(9×10¹⁶) = 8.20×10⁻¹⁴ J = 0.511 MeV

E = γmc² = (5/3)(0.511 MeV) ≈ 0.852 MeV

K = E − E₀ = 0.852 − 0.511 = 0.341 MeV
