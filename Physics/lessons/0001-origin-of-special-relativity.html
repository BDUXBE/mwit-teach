Origin of Special Relativity

By the end of this lesson you will be able to: distinguish inertial from non-inertial frames, state the Galilean transformation, explain why it breaks down for light, and recite Einstein's two postulates.

1. What is Relativity?

Relativity is the idea that measured values of physical quantities — position, distance, time, velocity — can differ depending on the motion of the observer who measures them.

Key question to keep in mind: Which things are absolute (same for everyone) and which are relative (depend on who is looking)?

2. Frames of Reference

Type Definition Example
Inertial frame Moves at constant velocity (no acceleration) Train at steady speed, spaceship coasting
Non-inertial frame Accelerating or rotating Car braking, spinning carousel

Newton's laws (and Galilean relativity) only work correctly inside inertial frames. This is the starting assumption of special relativity too.

3. Galilean Relativity

Galileo (and Newton) claimed: "All inertial observers see the same laws of physics, even though they measure different velocities." Time, however, was assumed to be absolute — everyone's clock ticks at the same rate.

Galilean Transformation Equations

Imagine two frames: the rest frame $O$ (you, standing still) and a moving frame $O'$ (a truck moving at speed $v$ along the $x$-axis). At $t = 0$ both origins coincide.

Galilean Transformation — from O to O′ x′ = x − vt
y′ = y
z′ = z
t′ = t    ← time is absolute in Galilean relativity
Galilean Velocity Addition u′ = u − v
(u = object speed in O, u′ = object speed in O′)

Example: You throw a ball at 10 m/s inside a train moving at 30 m/s. A person on the platform sees the ball at $10 + 30 = 40$ m/s. Makes sense — this is everyday experience.

4. The Crisis — Light Breaks Galilean Relativity

In the 1860s Maxwell derived that light (an electromagnetic wave) travels at:

Maxwell's Four Equations ∇·E = ρ/ε₀                (Gauss — charges create electric fields)
∇·B = 0                   (no magnetic monopoles)
∇×E = −∂B/∂t            (Faraday — changing B creates E)
∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t   (Ampère–Maxwell — changing E creates B)

Combining the two curl equations gives a wave equation with speed:
c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀) = 2.998 × 10⁸ m/s

The problem: Maxwell's equations give one specific number for the speed of light with no mention of a reference frame. Apply Galilean velocity addition:

Galilean prediction for light speed seen by moving observer c′ = c + v    (or c − v)
Contradiction: Galilean relativity says a moving observer should measure light at $c + v$ or $c - v$. But Maxwell says every observer should get exactly $c$. Both cannot be right.

Four possible explanations debated in the 1880s–1900s

  1. The principle of relativity itself is wrong.
  2. Maxwell's equations are wrong.
  3. The Galilean transformation is incomplete.
  4. Light travels through a mysterious medium called the Ether, so $c$ is only constant relative to it.

The Michelson–Morley Experiment (1887)

Michelson and Morley tried to detect the Earth's motion through the Ether by comparing light travel times in perpendicular directions. Result: no difference detected — no Ether wind. This demolished option 4.

How the Apparatus Worked A half-silvered mirror splits a beam into two perpendicular arms:

Arm 1 (parallel to Earth's motion): light travels at c+v outward, c−v returning.
Round-trip time: t₁ = 2Lc / (c²−v²) = (2L/c) · 1/(1−v²/c²)

Arm 2 (perpendicular): effective speed each way = √(c²−v²).
Round-trip time: t₂ = 2L/√(c²−v²) = (2L/c) · 1/√(1−v²/c²)

Classical prediction: t₁ ≠ t₂ → fringe shift visible in the interferometer.
Observed: zero fringe shift → t₁ = t₂ → no Ether wind detected.

Experiments showed Maxwell's equations were never wrong. That ruled out option 2.
Einstein chose to keep both the principle of relativity and Maxwell's constant $c$ — which forced him to discard the Galilean transformation (option 3) and revise our understanding of time.

5. Einstein's Two Postulates (1905)

Postulate 1 — The Principle of Relativity:
The laws of physics have the same form in all inertial frames of reference.
Postulate 2 — Constancy of the Speed of Light:
The speed of light in a vacuum is $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s in all inertial frames, regardless of the velocity of the source or the observer.

These two postulates together force a radical conclusion: time is not absolute. Two observers moving relative to each other will measure different time intervals and different lengths for the same events. This leads directly to time dilation and length contraction (Lesson 2).

Summary — Galilean vs Einsteinian Relativity

Property Galilean (Classical) Einsteinian (Special)
Time Absolute (same for all) Relative (depends on observer)
Space Absolute Relative
Speed of light Relative (c + v or c − v) Absolute (always c)
Valid when… $v \ll c$ (everyday speeds) All speeds up to $c$
