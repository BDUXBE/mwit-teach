The Lorentz Transformation

By the end of this lesson you will be able to: write the Lorentz transformation equations from memory, convert event coordinates between frames, apply the velocity addition formula, and explain how time dilation and length contraction emerge from the transformation.

1. The Setup

Frame $O$ (rest frame) and frame $O'$ (moving frame) have their origins coinciding at $t = t' = 0$. Frame $O'$ moves at constant speed $v$ in the positive $x$-direction relative to $O$.

An event (something that happens at one place and time) has coordinates $(x, y, z, t)$ in $O$ and $(x', y', z', t')$ in $O'$. The Lorentz transformation converts between them.

2. The Lorentz Transformation Equations

From O → O′ (observer in O measures event)
x′ = γ(x − vt)
y′ = y
z′ = z
t′ = γ(t − vx/c²)
⇄
From O′ → O (observer in O′ measures event)
x = γ(x′ + vt′)
y = y′
z = z′
t = γ(t′ + vx′/c²)
Pattern: Going O→O′ uses minus signs ($x - vt$, $t - vx/c^2$). Going O′→O uses plus signs ($x' + vt'$, $t' + vx'/c^2$). Only $x$ and $t$ mix — $y$ and $z$ are unchanged.
The $t'$ equation is the crucial one to memorize: $t' = \gamma(t - vx/c^2)$. The $vx/c^2$ term is what makes simultaneity relative — it shows that time in $O'$ depends on both $t$ AND the spatial position $x$ in $O$.
Reminder — Lorentz Factor γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²)    always ≥ 1

3. Connection to Galilean Transformation

When $v \ll c$, the term $vx/c^2 \approx 0$ and $\gamma \approx 1$, so:

x′ ≈ x − vt    (Galilean)
t′ ≈ t    (Galilean — time becomes absolute again)

This shows the Lorentz transformation contains Galilean relativity as a low-speed limit.

4. Relativistic Velocity Addition

If an object moves at velocity $u_x$ in frame $O$, what velocity $u_x'$ does it have in frame $O'$? Differentiating the Lorentz transformation gives:

Relativistic Velocity Addition (x-direction) u′ₓ = (uₓ − v) / (1 − uₓv/c²)     [O → O′]

uₓ = (u′ₓ + v) / (1 + u′ₓv/c²)     [O′ → O]
Test with light: Set $u_x = c$ in the O→O′ formula:
$u_x' = (c - v)/(1 - cv/c^2) = (c-v)/((c-v)/c) = c$ ✓ The speed of light is $c$ in every frame — Postulate 2 confirmed.

Transverse (y, z) Velocity Components

The x-direction formula above is the most tested, but the full transformation also applies to transverse velocities:

Transverse Velocity Transformation (O → O′) u′_y = u_y / [γ(1 − uₓv/c²)]
u′_z = u_z / [γ(1 − uₓv/c²)]

Special case — object moves purely in y direction (uₓ = 0):
u′_y = u_y / γ    ← transverse speed also reduces (by factor γ)
Why transverse speed changes too: Even though y′ = y (no length contraction perpendicular to motion), the time interval transforms. Since speed = distance/time, and time dilates, the transverse speed must shrink by γ.

Worked Example — from Lec03

Spaceship A moves at $0.75c$ relative to Earth. Spaceship B moves at $0.85c$ relative to Earth (same direction). What is the speed of B as seen from A?

v = 0.75c (speed of A = our moving frame O′)
uₓ = 0.85c (speed of B in Earth frame O)

u′ₓ = (0.85c − 0.75c) / (1 − (0.85)(0.75))
= 0.10c / (1 − 0.6375)
= 0.10c / 0.3625
≈ 0.276c

Classic (Galilean) answer would be $0.85c - 0.75c = 0.10c$. The relativistic answer is $0.276c$ — larger than the Galilean result but still less than $c$.

5. Length Contraction of an Inclined Rod

Length contraction only applies to the component along the direction of motion. If a rod is tilted at angle θ₀ (proper angle) in the rest frame O, and frame O′ moves along x at speed v, then in O′:

Inclined Rod — Component Contraction Rest frame O:   Lₓ = L₀ cos θ₀   (x-component)    L_y = L₀ sin θ₀   (y-component)

Moving frame O′:
L′ₓ = Lₓ/γ = L₀ cos θ₀ / γ    (x contracts)
L′_y = L_y = L₀ sin θ₀         (y unchanged)

Observed length:   L′ = √(L′ₓ² + L′_y²)
Observed angle:    tan θ′ = L′_y / L′ₓ = γ tan θ₀    (angle increases)
Result: In the moving frame the rod appears both shorter AND more steeply tilted. Only the x-component shrinks — the y-component is unchanged.

6. Recovering Time Dilation from Lorentz

Suppose a clock sits at rest in $O'$ ($x' = 0$ always). Two ticks happen at $t_1'$ and $t_2'$ — proper time interval $\Delta t_0 = t_2' - t_1'$. Using the inverse transform $t = \gamma(t' + vx'/c^2)$ with $x' = 0$:

Δt = γ · Δt₀    ← time dilation falls directly out of Lorentz ✓

7. Step-by-Step Method for Lorentz Problems

  1. Label the frames: which is $O$ and which is $O'$? What is $v$?
  2. Identify the event's known coordinates.
  3. Choose the right direction (O→O′ uses minus; O′→O uses plus).
  4. Compute γ.
  5. Plug in and calculate.

Check Your Understanding

Q1. Two events happen at the same time $t$ in frame $O$, but at different positions $x_1$ and $x_2$. What does the Lorentz transformation tell us about their times in $O'$?
Q2. At $t = 0$ an event occurs at $x = 5$ m in frame $O$. Frame $O'$ moves at a speed giving $\gamma = 2$. What is $x'$?
Q3. A probe moves at $0.60c$ in frame $O$. Frame $O'$ moves at $0.80c$ in the same direction. What is the probe's speed in $O'$?
Q4. In what limit do the Lorentz equations reduce to the Galilean transformation?
