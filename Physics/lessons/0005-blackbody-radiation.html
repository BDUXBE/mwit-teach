Blackbody Radiation & Planck's Quantum Theory

By the end of this lesson you will be able to: apply the Stefan–Boltzmann law and Wien's displacement law to solve blackbody problems, explain the ultraviolet catastrophe, and describe how Planck's quantization resolves it.

1. The Bridge from Relativity to Quantum

By 1900, two things were certain: (1) atoms exist, (2) classical physics completely failed to explain how atoms radiate light. The crisis began with the blackbody.

Problems with classical atomic models (Rutherford's planetary model, 1911):

  • Classical electrodynamics predicts an orbiting electron should radiate energy continuously and spiral into the nucleus in ~10−10 s. Atoms are stable — so something is wrong.
  • It cannot explain discrete spectral lines (only certain frequencies emitted).

2. What is a Blackbody?

Blackbody: An idealized object that absorbs all incoming radiation (reflects nothing) and emits thermal radiation whose spectrum depends only on its temperature, not its material.

Real-world examples: a furnace cavity, the Sun (approximate), a perfect black surface.

3. Stefan–Boltzmann Law — Total Power Radiated

Stefan–Boltzmann Law P = εσAT⁴

σ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W·m⁻²·K⁻⁴    (Stefan–Boltzmann constant)
ε = emissivity (0 to 1; perfect blackbody has ε = 1)
A = surface area (m²)
T = absolute temperature (K)

Key insight: Total radiated power scales as $T^4$ — double the temperature, and you get 16× more power.

Emissivity of Real Materials

MaterialEmissivity ε
Perfect blackbody (ideal)1.00
Human skin0.97–0.98
Brick / concrete0.90–0.95
Glass0.90
Aluminum (oxidized)0.30
Polished aluminum0.05
Polished silver0.02

Thermal cameras and infrared thermometers rely on accurate ε values. Human skin is nearly a perfect emitter — that's why IR thermometers work well for body temperature.

4. Wien's Displacement Law — Peak Wavelength

Wien's Displacement Law λ_max · T = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K

λ_max = 2.898 × 10⁻³ / T    (in metres, T in Kelvin)
Meaning: Hotter objects peak at shorter wavelengths (bluer light). Cooler objects peak at longer wavelengths (redder/infrared). This is why hot metal glows red → orange → white → blue-white as temperature increases.

Worked Example — Sun's Color

Surface temperature of Sun ≈ 5500 K:

λ_max = 2.898 × 10⁻³ / 5500 ≈ 5.27 × 10⁻⁷ m = 527 nm → green light
(The Sun emits across the full visible spectrum; we perceive the mix as white/yellow)

5. The Ultraviolet Catastrophe

Rayleigh and Jeans derived a classical formula for blackbody radiation using Maxwell's equations + Boltzmann's equipartition theorem:

Rayleigh–Jeans Law (Classical) u(f, T) = (8πf²/c³) · kT

u = energy density per unit frequency  |  k = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K (Boltzmann constant)
The catastrophe: Rayleigh–Jeans correctly matches experimental data at low frequencies, but as frequency increases, the formula predicts $u(f,T) \to \infty$. A real blackbody would radiate infinite energy — obviously wrong. This failure at high frequencies (ultraviolet and beyond) was called the ultraviolet catastrophe.

6. Planck's Quantum Hypothesis (1900)

Max Planck proposed a radical new assumption: the energy of atomic oscillators is not continuous — it comes in discrete packets called quanta.

Planck's Energy Quantization E = nhf    (n = 1, 2, 3, …)

h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s = 4.136 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV·s    (Planck's constant)
f = oscillation frequency
n = positive integer (quantum number)
Why this fixes the catastrophe: At high frequencies, the energy gap between levels ($hf$) becomes very large. Thermal energy ($\sim kT$) cannot bridge this gap, so high-frequency modes are effectively "frozen out." The infinite blowup never happens.

Where Planck's Formula Comes From — Replacing the Integral with a Sum

Classical physics uses the Boltzmann distribution to find the average energy of an oscillator at temperature T. The classical (continuous) version gives:

Classical average energy (continuous — wrong at high f) Ē = ∫₀^∞ E · e^(−E/kT) dE  /  ∫₀^∞ e^(−E/kT) dE = kT

Every mode gets the same average energy kT — regardless of frequency. → catastrophe.

Planck replaced the continuous integral with a discrete sum over allowed energies E = 0, hf, 2hf, 3hf, …:

Planck's discrete average energy (correct) Ē = Σ(n=0→∞) nhf · e^(−nhf/kT)  /  Σ(n=0→∞) e^(−nhf/kT)

Result:   Ē = hf / (e^(hf/kT) − 1)

Limits:
  hf ≪ kT (low f):   Ē ≈ kT    → recovers classical result ✓
  hf ≫ kT (high f):   Ē → 0    → high-frequency modes "frozen out" ✓

Planck's Radiation Formula

Planck's Law — Frequency Form u(f, T) = (8πhf³/c³) · 1/(e^(hf/kT) − 1)

Low f limit: e^(hf/kT) ≈ 1 + hf/kT, so denominator → hf/kT, and u(f,T) → (8πf²/c³)·kT = Rayleigh–Jeans ✓
Planck's Law — Wavelength Form (matches experimental graphs) I(λ, T) = (2πhc²/λ⁵) · 1/(e^(hc/λkT) − 1)

λ_max from Wien's law: set dI/dλ = 0 → λ_max · T = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K ✓

7. Summary — Classical vs Quantum Picture

Aspect Classical (Rayleigh–Jeans) Quantum (Planck)
Energy of oscillator Any continuous value Only $E = nhf$ (discrete)
Prediction at low $f$ Correct Correct
Prediction at high $f$ → ∞ (catastrophe) → 0 (correct)
Total energy radiated Infinite Finite (matches $P = \sigma T^4$)
