Time Dilation & Length Contraction

By the end of this lesson you will be able to: define proper time and proper length, apply the time dilation and length contraction formulas, and use the Lorentz factor $\gamma$ correctly in calculations.

1. The Lorentz Factor γ (gamma)

Both time dilation and length contraction depend on the same factor $\gamma$ (pronounced "gamma"), which appears everywhere in special relativity:

Lorentz Factor γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)    where β = v/c

γ = 1 / √(1 − β²)

Key properties of γ:

2. Time Dilation — Moving Clocks Run Slow

Where the Formula Comes From — The Light Clock

Imagine a clock made of two mirrors with a light pulse bouncing between them. In the rest frame the pulse travels straight up and down, covering distance $d$ each way. One tick = time for one round trip: $\Delta t_0 = 2d/c$.

Now watch the same clock from a frame where it moves horizontally at speed $v$. The pulse still travels at $c$ (Postulate 2), but now traces a diagonal path. In the time $\Delta t/2$ for one half-trip, the clock moves $v \Delta t/2$ sideways. By Pythagoras:

Light-Clock Derivation Diagonal path length = √( d² + (vΔt/2)² ) = c · Δt/2

Squaring:   d² + v²Δt²/4 = c²Δt²/4
But d = cΔt₀/2, so:   c²Δt₀²/4 + v²Δt²/4 = c²Δt²/4

Rearranging:   Δt² (c² − v²) = c² Δt₀²
∴   Δt = Δt₀ / √(1 − v²/c²) = γ Δt₀
Key insight: The derivation uses only Pythagoras and the constancy of $c$. No extra assumptions — time dilation is a direct, unavoidable consequence of Postulate 2.

Proper Time $\Delta t_0$

Proper time ($\Delta t_0$) is the time interval measured by a clock that is at rest relative to the event — i.e., the clock is present at both the start and end of the event. It is the shortest possible time any observer can measure for that event.

Time Dilation — Δt in frame where the clock moves Δt = γ · Δt₀

Since γ ≥ 1, Δt ≥ Δt₀ → the moving clock appears to tick slower
Memory trick: "Moving clocks run slow."
The observer who sees the clock moving always measures a longer time than the clock itself.

Example — Muon Survival

Muons are unstable particles created by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere (~15 km up). They decay radioactively with a proper lifetime $\tau = 2.2\;\mu\text{s}$, meaning the number surviving after time $t$ (in the muon's own rest frame) follows:

Radioactive Decay Law N(t) = N₀ · e^(−t/τ)     τ = 2.2 μs (mean lifetime of muons at rest)

Muons travel at $v = 0.9978c$ (lecture value). Their proper lifetime is $\tau = 2.2\;\mu\text{s}$.

Classical prediction — distance before most muons decay: $d \approx v \cdot \tau = (0.9978c)(2.2\times10^{-6}) \approx 0.66$ km → should not reach Earth.

Relativistic prediction — $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-0.9978^2} \approx 15$:

Δt = γ · τ = 15 × 2.2 μs ≈ 33 μs    (dilated lifetime in Earth frame)
d = v · Δt ≈ (0.9978c)(33 μs) ≈ 9.9 km — muons DO reach Earth ✓

Observed survival rate matches N(t) = N₀ e^(−t_Earth / γτ) — experiment confirmed.

3. Length Contraction — Moving Objects Are Shorter

Proper Length $L_0$

Proper length ($L_0$) is the length of an object measured in the frame where it is at rest. It is the longest length any observer measures.

Length Contraction — L in frame where the object moves L = L₀ / γ    (equivalently: L = L₀ · √(1 − v²/c²))

Since γ ≥ 1, L ≤ L₀ → moving objects appear shorter
Important: Length contraction only occurs along the direction of motion. Dimensions perpendicular to motion are unchanged: $y' = y$, $z' = z$.

Example — Muon from the muon's frame

From the muon's own frame, the muon is at rest but the atmosphere contracts. The proper length of the atmosphere path is $L_0 = 15$ km. At $\gamma = 7.09$:

L = L₀/γ = 15 km / 7.09 ≈ 2.1 km — easily traversed in 2.2 μs ✓

Both perspectives (time dilation in ground frame, length contraction in muon frame) give the same physical result.

4. Relativity of Simultaneity

Two events that are simultaneous in one frame are generally not simultaneous in another frame moving relative to the first. This is a direct consequence of Postulate 2 (constant $c$).

Rule of thumb: Events separated in space can only be simultaneous for one particular frame. Other frames disagree on which came first. This only matters at speeds comparable to $c$.

5. Exam Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify which frame is the "proper" frame (clock is at rest, or object is at rest).
  2. Calculate γ from the given speed $v$.
  3. Apply the right formula:
    Time dilation: $\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$ (proper time is always shorter)
    Length contraction: $L = L_0/\gamma$ (proper length is always longer)
  4. Sanity check: Is your answer physically reasonable? ($\Delta t > \Delta t_0$? $L < L_0$?)

Check Your Understanding

Q1. A spaceship travels from Earth to a distant star. Which observer measures the proper time for the trip?
Q2. A spaceship moves at $v = 0.8c$. The on-board clock shows 10 years have passed. How long did Earth observers measure?
Q3. A rocket of proper length 1000 m flies past you at $v = 0.8c$. What length do you measure?
Q4. A cube moves at $v = 0.95c$ horizontally. Which dimensions are contracted?
